# Universal Communicative Intents: A Cross-Linguistic Master Database
### A Notional-Functional Framework for Language-Learning Syllabus Design

> **Design Philosophy:** Every entry in this database is language-agnostic. The Structural Semantic Blueprint describes abstract psychological slots — not syntax — so the framework applies equally to Mandarin, Swahili, Finnish, Arabic, or any other human language. The goal is to map 100% of the communicative intents required to navigate human life.

---

## How to Read Each Entry

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Core Human Intent** | The universal communicative act being performed |
| **Structural Semantic Blueprint** | Abstract psychological slots required to execute the intent |
| **Pragmatic Context** | When, why, and in what social hierarchy this pattern is deployed |
| **Universal Target Scenarios** | Three real-world situations requiring this exact intent |

# MODULE 1: CORE TRANSACTIONS & PHYSICAL LOGISTICS

*The foundational layer of human communication — getting things, going places, solving practical problems.*

---

## 1.1 Sourcing & Acquisition

---

### Intent 1.1.1 — Requesting the Existence or Location of an Asset

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Politeness Opener] + [Target Asset] + [Location/Source Indicator] + [Optional: Specificity Qualifier]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Used when the speaker lacks knowledge of where to find a physical or informational resource. Power dynamic is typically neutral or slightly deferential (speaker to service provider, stranger, or authority). Urgency level and formality adjust the degree of hedging in the opener.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A traveler arriving at a large transit hub needs to find the ticket office, bathroom, or departure platform.
- Scenario B: A patient entering a hospital complex for the first time needs to locate the correct department or ward.
- Scenario C: A new employee on their first day needs to find the HR office, server room, or supplies cabinet.

---

### Intent 1.1.2 — Inquiring About Availability

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Target Asset/Person/Time Slot] + [Availability Probe] + [Optional: Time Frame Qualifier]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Used before committing to a transaction or plan. Speaker is in a tentative, pre-decision state. Socially, it signals respect for the other party's resources or schedule. Common in commercial, professional, and social planning contexts.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A customer at a market or shop asking whether a specific product is in stock.
- Scenario B: A person contacting a professional (doctor, mechanic, lawyer) to ask if they have appointment slots open.
- Scenario C: Someone asking a friend or colleague whether they are free at a specific time before proposing plans.

---

### Intent 1.1.3 — Requesting the Price or Cost of Something

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Politeness Opener] + [Target Asset/Service] + [Cost Inquiry Marker] + [Optional: Unit/Quantity Qualifier]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Pre-transactional information gathering. Speaker holds consumer power; the other party holds asset power. Cultural norms vary widely on directness — some cultures treat price inquiry as neutral, others as an implicit negotiation opener.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A customer at a food stall asking the price before ordering.
- Scenario B: A person calling a repair service to get an estimate before agreeing to a job.
- Scenario C: A traveler at a currency exchange asking the current rate.

---

### Intent 1.1.4 — Placing a Specific Order or Request for Service

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Volition Marker] + [Specific Asset/Service] + [Quantity/Specification] + [Optional: Condition or Customization]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The speaker has moved from inquiry to decision. This is an act of commitment. Formality scales with the context — ordering at a restaurant vs. placing a formal procurement order. The hearer is now obligated to fulfill the request.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Ordering a specific dish with dietary modifications at a restaurant.
- Scenario B: Placing a custom order with a craftsperson or tailor — specifying material, size, and deadline.
- Scenario C: Formally requesting a specific document or report from an administrative office.

---

### Intent 1.1.5 — Negotiating Price or Terms

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of Offer] + [Resistance/Incompatibility Marker] + [Counter-Proposal] + [Optional: Justification]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A face-threatening act for both parties. Speaker challenges the hearer's pricing power while the hearer defends it. Culturally variable — expected and normal in many markets, considered rude in others. Requires careful calibration of directness vs. softening.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A buyer at an open-air market countering a vendor's initial price.
- Scenario B: A freelancer negotiating their rate with a prospective client.
- Scenario C: A tenant asking a landlord to lower rent or include utilities in the price.

---

### Intent 1.1.6 — Requesting a Replacement, Exchange, or Refund

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Problem Statement] + [Evidence/Justification] + [Desired Resolution] + [Optional: Reference to Rights or Policy]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker is in a position of legitimate grievance. Success depends on asserting rights without aggression. The hearer (service provider) must balance policy with customer relations. Passive or aggressive delivery both tend to fail — assertive directness is most universally effective.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Returning a defective product to a shop and requesting an exchange or refund.
- Scenario B: Asking a restaurant to replace a dish that was wrong or unsatisfactory.
- Scenario C: Requesting a refund from an online service after a billing error.

---

### Intent 1.1.7 — Borrowing or Requesting Temporary Use of an Asset

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Politeness Opener] + [Target Asset] + [Temporary Use Marker] + [Time Frame] + [Optional: Promise of Return]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Involves transfer of temporary possession without transfer of ownership. Creates a social debt. The degree of hedging required scales with the value of the asset and the closeness of the relationship. Failing to return creates interpersonal friction.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking a neighbor to borrow a tool needed for a short home repair job.
- Scenario B: Asking a colleague to use their computer or phone temporarily.
- Scenario C: Asking a friend to lend money with an explicit promise of repayment.

## 1.2 Spatial Orientation & Movement

---

### Intent 1.2.1 — Asking for Directions

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Attention-Getting Opener] + [Current Position Acknowledgment] + [Destination Marker] + [Path Inquiry]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker is spatially disoriented or uncertain. Hearer is cast as a local authority. The interaction is brief and transactional. Cultural norms affect whether strangers are expected to help, how much detail is appropriate, and whether physical gestures accompany the verbal response.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A visitor in an unfamiliar city asking a passerby how to reach a landmark or address.
- Scenario B: A patient in a large hospital asking a staff member which corridor leads to a specific ward.
- Scenario C: A driver who is lost asking for directions to return to a main road.

---

### Intent 1.2.2 — Giving Directions

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Starting Reference Point] + [Sequential Movement Instructions] + [Landmark Anchors] + [Destination Confirmation]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker takes on temporary authority and responsibility for the hearer's navigation. Accuracy is paramount — errors have real consequences. Good direction-givers use landmarks, not just compass directions, as landmarks are universally more cognitively accessible.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Explaining to a stranger how to walk from a bus stop to a local market.
- Scenario B: Telling a delivery person the exact route to a difficult-to-find address.
- Scenario C: Guiding someone by phone who is lost and navigating in real time.

---

### Intent 1.2.3 — Stating a Destination or Announcing Movement

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Subject/Agent] + [Movement Verb Slot] + [Destination] + [Optional: Time Frame or Purpose]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker informs others of intended or current movement. Serves coordination functions in groups or families. Also used when purchasing transport — telling a taxi driver, bus conductor, or booking agent where you are going.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a taxi or rideshare driver your destination at the start of a journey.
- Scenario B: Informing family members where you are going before leaving the house.
- Scenario C: Telling a travel agent the origin and destination of a trip you want to book.

---

### Intent 1.2.4 — Describing a Location or Physical Space

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Reference Anchor Point] + [Spatial Relationship Marker] + [Target Location Description] + [Optional: Identifying Features]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Used when the speaker must help a hearer mentally construct a spatial picture. Requires the speaker to take the hearer's perspective — using their reference point, not the speaker's. Fails when speakers describe from their own current position without making the anchor explicit.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Describing where your house is located to someone who has never visited.
- Scenario B: Explaining where a specific item is located within a room or building.
- Scenario C: Describing the layout of a venue to someone planning to attend an event there.

## 1.3 Time, Scheduling & Appointments

---

### Intent 1.3.1 — Stating a Specific Time or Date

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Event/Action] + [Temporal Anchor] + [Optional: Duration or Frequency]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Fundamental to coordination. The speaker takes responsibility for temporal accuracy. Misunderstandings at this level (AM/PM, date format conventions, time zone differences) have high real-world costs.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Confirming the time of a meeting with a colleague.
- Scenario B: Telling a patient when their next appointment is scheduled.
- Scenario C: Arranging a specific time for a service provider to arrive at your home.

---

### Intent 1.3.2 — Making an Appointment or Reservation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Volition Marker] + [Service/Space/Person] + [Desired Time Slot] + [Optional: Duration or Special Requirements]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A speech act that creates a mutual social obligation. Both parties are now committed. The speaker secures access; the hearer reserves resources. Cancellation without notice violates a social contract and may carry penalties.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Booking a table at a restaurant for a specific number of people at a specific time.
- Scenario B: Scheduling a medical or dental appointment by phone.
- Scenario C: Reserving a hotel room for specific dates, including check-in and check-out.

---

### Intent 1.3.3 — Changing or Cancelling an Appointment

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Reference to Existing Commitment] + [Change/Cancellation Marker] + [Reason/Apology] + [Optional: Alternative Proposal]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A face-threatening act — the speaker is breaking a prior commitment. Mitigation is essential: acknowledgment of inconvenience, a valid reason, and an alternative offer significantly reduce social damage. The less notice given, the more mitigation is required.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Calling a clinic to reschedule a medical appointment due to a schedule conflict.
- Scenario B: Informing a service provider that you need to postpone a home visit.
- Scenario C: Telling a friend you can no longer make a planned social event.

---

### Intent 1.3.4 — Inquiring About Duration or How Long Something Takes

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Process/Activity/Journey] + [Duration Inquiry Marker] + [Optional: Start/End Reference Point]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker needs to plan or set expectations. Common in service contexts (waiting times), travel (journey length), and project management. The answer sets an implicit contract — if the actual duration significantly exceeds the stated one, a second speech act of explanation is required.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking a mechanic how long a car repair will take.
- Scenario B: Asking a transit official how long the journey to a destination is.
- Scenario C: Asking a colleague how long a task or process typically takes.

## 1.4 Permissions, Rules & Procedures

---

### Intent 1.4.1 — Requesting Permission

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Politeness Opener] + [Proposed Action] + [Permission Probe] + [Optional: Justification]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker acknowledges the hearer's authority over a space, resource, or decision. Fundamental to hierarchical relationships (employee to manager, citizen to official, child to parent). The degree of hedging signals the speaker's awareness of the potential imposition.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking a supervisor for permission to leave work early for a personal reason.
- Scenario B: Asking a property owner if you may use a space, path, or facility.
- Scenario C: Asking another person at a public location if a seat near them is taken.

---

### Intent 1.4.2 — Granting Permission

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Affirmative Marker] + [Optional: Condition or Constraint] + [Optional: Encouragement]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker exercises legitimate authority. A simple grant is the minimal form. Adding conditions ("yes, but...") softens absolute authority while still controlling the outcome. Overly elaborate conditions on minor requests can feel controlling and damage rapport.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A manager approving an employee's request for a day off.
- Scenario B: A homeowner telling a guest they are welcome to use a particular room or item.
- Scenario C: A teacher allowing a student to leave class early.

---

### Intent 1.4.3 — Denying Permission

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Negative Marker] + [Reason/Policy Reference] + [Optional: Alternative or Partial Concession]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker exercises authority to restrict. The reason provided is critical — an unexplained denial feels arbitrary and triggers resistance. A policy or external constraint ("it's the rules") is less face-threatening than a personal decision ("I don't want you to"). Offering an alternative softens the refusal.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A security officer telling a visitor they cannot enter a restricted area.
- Scenario B: A landlord telling a tenant they cannot keep a pet in the property.
- Scenario C: A parent telling a child they cannot attend an event or activity.

---

### Intent 1.4.4 — Explaining a Rule, Policy, or Procedure

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Rule/Policy Statement] + [Scope Marker] + [Reason or Origin] + [Optional: Consequence of Non-Compliance]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker transmits institutional or social norms. Speaker is positioned as a representative of a system, not as an individual. Clarity and neutrality are valued — personalizing a rule ("I want you to...") can backfire. Used heavily by service workers, officials, educators, and managers.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A bank employee explaining the procedure for opening a new account.
- Scenario B: A customs official explaining what items must be declared at the border.
- Scenario C: An HR representative explaining the company's leave policy to a new employee.

---

### Intent 1.4.5 — Asking for Instructions on How to Do Something

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Target Process/Task] + [Instruction Inquiry Marker] + [Optional: Current Knowledge Disclosure]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker admits a competence gap and casts the hearer as a more knowledgeable guide. This requires some vulnerability. In high-power-distance cultures, admitting you don't know how to do something can be face-threatening; in low-power-distance cultures, it is entirely normal and pragmatically efficient.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A new employee asking a colleague how to use an internal system or process.
- Scenario B: A customer asking a pharmacist how and when to take a prescribed medication.
- Scenario C: A traveler asking a local how to use the public transport ticketing system.

*The relational layer — managing emotions, relationships, obligations, and conflict across all social hierarchies.*

## 2.1 Expressing & Managing Social Obligations

---

### Intent 2.1.1 — Stating an Unavoidable Obligation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Agent] + [High-Necessity Modal Slot] + [Required Action] + [Optional: Timeframe or Consequence of Non-Compliance]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker frames an action as non-negotiable, driven by external or internal necessity. This distances the speaker from personal agency ("I want to") and positions the action as imposed by circumstance, duty, or rule. Used to explain why one cannot do something else, or to instruct others.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Explaining to a friend that you cannot join them because you are required to attend a mandatory work event.
- Scenario B: A parent explaining to a child that they must complete homework before any leisure activity.
- Scenario C: A patient explaining to a host that they must take medication at a strict time every day.

---

### Intent 2.1.2 — Expressing a Desire or Preference

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Agent] + [Desire/Preference Marker] + [Desired Object/Outcome] + [Optional: Contrast with What Is Not Desired]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Positions the speaker as having a personal stake or inclination. Lower stakes than an obligation. Used to guide decisions in groups, express personal taste, or signal what would make someone happy. Desire expressions invite negotiation; obligation expressions do not.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Expressing a preference for a window seat when checking in for a flight.
- Scenario B: Telling a host what kind of food you enjoy when asked about dinner preferences.
- Scenario C: Telling a group which option you prefer when a collective decision is being made.

---

### Intent 2.1.3 — Making a Suggestion or Recommendation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Softener/Hedge] + [Proposed Action or Option] + [Optional: Justification or Benefit]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker offers input without imposing. The hedge is critical — it signals that the speaker respects the hearer's autonomy. A suggestion stated too directly becomes a command. Recommendations carry slightly more authority because the speaker implies expertise or experience.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Recommending a dish to a friend who is unsure what to order.
- Scenario B: Suggesting to a colleague a more efficient approach to a task they are struggling with.
- Scenario C: A doctor recommending lifestyle changes to a patient without prescribing them as mandatory.

---

### Intent 2.1.4 — Offering Help or Assistance

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Observation of Need / Proactive Stance] + [Offer Marker] + [Specific Assistance Available] + [Optional: Condition or Constraint]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker takes initiative to reduce the hearer's burden. The offer should be specific rather than vague — vague offers ("let me know if you need anything") are culturally valued in some contexts as polite signals, but specific offers ("I can drive you there") are more actionable and sincere. Declining an offer also requires a specific speech act (see 2.2.3).

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Offering to carry heavy bags for someone who is visibly struggling.
- Scenario B: Offering to cover a colleague's responsibilities while they deal with a personal emergency.
- Scenario C: Offering a friend help with a task they mentioned finding difficult.

---

### Intent 2.1.5 — Accepting an Offer or Invitation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Affirmative Marker] + [Optional: Expression of Gratitude] + [Optional: Confirmation of Details]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The speaker aligns with the hearer's proposal, creating mutual commitment. An enthusiastic acceptance strengthens the relationship; a reluctant or heavily qualified acceptance can inadvertently signal ambivalence, which may offend even when acceptance is genuine.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Accepting an invitation to a social event with enthusiasm.
- Scenario B: Accepting a colleague's offer to help with a project.
- Scenario C: Accepting a formal job offer from an employer.

---

## 2.2 Refusal, Deflection & Boundary Management

---

### Intent 2.2.1 — Declining an Invitation (Soft Refusal)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Expression of Appreciation for Offer] + [Regret Marker] + [Incompatibility/Obstacle] + [Optional: Future Alternative]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
One of the most culturally variable speech acts. Some cultures require elaborate justification; others accept a simple "no thank you." The softening sequence (appreciation → regret → reason → alternative) is nearly universal in preserving face for both parties. Omitting the appreciation marker makes the refusal feel cold.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Declining a neighbor's invitation to a social gathering due to a prior commitment.
- Scenario B: Softly refusing a street vendor's persistent sales pitch without causing offense.
- Scenario C: Declining a second helping of food from a generous host without implying the food was unwelcome.

---

### Intent 2.2.2 — Declining a Request (Assertive Refusal)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of Request] + [Clear Negative Marker] + [Reason or Boundary Statement] + [Optional: Alternative Offer]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
More direct than a soft refusal. The speaker asserts a boundary or limitation. The challenge is to be clear without being harsh. In high-context cultures, stating reasons is essential; in low-context cultures, "no" alone is more socially acceptable. Offering an alternative partially compensates for the denial.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Declining a coworker's request to take on additional work that falls outside your role.
- Scenario B: Refusing a family member's request for a loan you are unable or unwilling to give.
- Scenario C: Telling a salesperson you are not interested in a product they are pushing aggressively.

---

### Intent 2.2.3 — Declining an Offer (Polite Rejection of Help or Gift)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Gratitude Marker] + [Negative Marker] + [Reason or Self-Sufficiency Signal] + [Optional: Reassurance]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Refusing an offer is not rude, but the manner matters greatly. In many cultures, a first refusal is expected (false modesty) and the offer is repeated. In others, the first "no" is taken at face value. The speaker must signal genuine sufficiency, not ingratitude.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Politely refusing a host's offer of more food when you are genuinely full.
- Scenario B: Declining a colleague's offer to help with a task you prefer to handle yourself.
- Scenario C: Refusing a gift that is inappropriate in scale or context without offending the giver.

---

### Intent 2.2.4 — Setting a Personal Boundary

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Statement of Limit/Boundary] + [Optional: Reason] + [Optional: Consequence of Violation] + [Optional: Alternative Acceptable Behavior]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
High-stakes interpersonal act. The speaker defines a non-negotiable limit on what they will accept. Clarity is more important than softening here — excessive hedging makes the boundary sound negotiable. Cultural context determines how explicitly boundaries can be stated without damaging the relationship.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a colleague that discussing your personal life at work is not something you are comfortable with.
- Scenario B: Informing a family member that a specific topic is off-limits in conversation.
- Scenario C: Telling a romantic partner that a certain behavior is unacceptable to you.

---

### Intent 2.2.5 — Deflecting an Unwanted Question

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of Question] + [Deflection Marker] + [Optional: Redirect or Topic Change]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker chooses not to answer without explicitly refusing. Deflection is more socially elegant than refusal in many cultures. Effective deflectors shift attention (to a related topic, to the asker's situation) rather than simply shutting the conversation down. Used to protect privacy, avoid conflict, or maintain professionalism.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: A public figure deflecting a journalist's question about a sensitive personal matter.
- Scenario B: Redirecting a relative who is asking about your income, relationship status, or life plans.
- Scenario C: A job candidate deflecting a question that is legally inappropriate in an interview.

## 2.3 Apology, Forgiveness & Repair

---

### Intent 2.3.1 — Apologizing (Minor Infraction)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Apology Marker] + [Acknowledgment of Specific Act] + [Optional: Brief Explanation]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Low-stakes social lubricant. Used to repair minor friction — accidentally bumping someone, being slightly late, a small mistake. Over-elaborating a minor apology can paradoxically draw more attention to the infraction. Speed and sincerity matter more than length.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Apologizing for accidentally interrupting someone mid-sentence.
- Scenario B: Saying sorry to a stranger after a minor physical collision in a public space.
- Scenario C: Apologizing to a colleague for a small error that caused minor inconvenience.

---

### Intent 2.3.2 — Apologizing (Serious Infraction)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Sincere Apology Marker] + [Full Acknowledgment of Impact] + [Responsibility Acceptance — No Deflection] + [Statement of Change or Remedy] + [Optional: Request for Forgiveness]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A morally weighted speech act. The key pragmatic failure is deflecting responsibility ("I'm sorry you felt that way"). An effective serious apology acknowledges the impact from the hearer's perspective, not just the speaker's intent. The promise of change is what distinguishes a genuine apology from a strategic one.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Apologizing to a close friend after a serious betrayal of trust.
- Scenario B: A professional formally apologizing to a client whose project was significantly mishandled.
- Scenario C: Apologizing to a family member for a hurtful statement made during an argument.

---

### Intent 2.3.3 — Accepting an Apology

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment Marker] + [Optional: Validation of Feeling] + [Forgiveness Signal] + [Optional: Forward-Looking Statement]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Completes the repair sequence. The speaker's choices carry weight: unconditional acceptance restores full harmony; conditional acceptance signals the relationship is damaged but repairable; refusal signals the harm was too serious to quickly forgive. All three are socially valid in appropriate contexts.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Accepting a friend's apology for cancelling plans at short notice.
- Scenario B: Accepting a colleague's apology for a miscommunication that caused extra work.
- Scenario C: Telling someone their apology is heard but that you need more time before fully forgiving.

---

### Intent 2.3.4 — Expressing Forgiveness Proactively

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Minimization of Harm or Acknowledgment of Good Intent] + [Forgiveness Declaration] + [Optional: Reassurance of Relationship Continuity]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker chooses to release the hearer from guilt without waiting to be asked. A generous act that signals high relational value. Can feel patronizing if the hearer didn't realize they caused harm, so awareness of the hearer's state is important.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a friend not to worry about a small mistake before they have a chance to apologize.
- Scenario B: Reassuring a new employee who made an honest error that it is not a serious issue.
- Scenario C: Proactively telling a partner that an earlier conflict is resolved and you are not holding onto it.

---

## 2.4 Gratitude, Compliments & Social Affirmation

---

### Intent 2.4.1 — Expressing Gratitude

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Gratitude Marker] + [Specific Act or Gift Acknowledged] + [Optional: Impact Statement]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Fundamental positive face act. Specificity transforms a generic "thank you" into a meaningful acknowledgment. The impact statement ("it really helped me") signals that the hearer's action had genuine value. Cultural norms vary on how elaborately gratitude should be expressed.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Thanking a colleague who stayed late to help you meet a deadline.
- Scenario B: Expressing gratitude to a host after staying in their home.
- Scenario C: Thanking a stranger who gave you directions that proved accurate and helpful.

---

### Intent 2.4.2 — Responding to Gratitude

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Minimization or Acknowledgment] + [Optional: Reaffirmation of Willingness to Help]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The completer of the gratitude exchange. Direct acceptance of thanks ("You're welcome") is standard in some cultures; minimization ("It was nothing") is more valued in others as a show of modesty. Awkwardly accepting praise by simply repeating "thank you" loops the exchange unnaturally.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Responding when a colleague thanks you for covering their responsibilities.
- Scenario B: Responding to a customer's thanks for good service.
- Scenario C: Responding when a friend expresses deep gratitude for emotional support.

---

### Intent 2.4.3 — Giving a Compliment

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Target of Compliment — Person/Object/Achievement] + [Positive Evaluation Marker] + [Optional: Specific Detail that Proves Sincerity]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A positive face-enhancing act. Specificity is the marker of sincerity — generic compliments ("nice job") carry less weight than specific ones ("the way you handled that difficult customer was really impressive"). Compliments on appearance must be calibrated carefully for power dynamics and cultural context.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Complimenting a colleague on a presentation that was particularly well-structured.
- Scenario B: Telling a host that their home is beautifully arranged or that the food was exceptional.
- Scenario C: Complimenting a friend on a decision or achievement that required courage.

---

### Intent 2.4.4 — Receiving a Compliment

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment Marker] + [Optional: Modest Deflection or Shared Credit] + [Optional: Reciprocal Positive Statement]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Culturally one of the most variable speech acts. Western norms tend toward direct acceptance ("Thank you, I worked hard on it"). Many East Asian, Middle Eastern, and Latin cultures favor deflection or modest denial ("Oh, it's nothing special"). Accepting too enthusiastically can read as arrogant; denying too strongly can invalidate the compliment-giver.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Responding when a manager praises your work performance in front of the team.
- Scenario B: Receiving a compliment on your appearance from a friend or acquaintance.
- Scenario C: Responding when someone praises a creative work you produced.

---

## 2.5 Empathy, Support & Emotional Mirroring

---

### Intent 2.5.1 — Expressing Sympathy or Condolence

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of Negative Event] + [Empathy Marker] + [Optional: Validation of Emotion] + [Optional: Offer of Support]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Used when the hearer has experienced loss, hardship, or pain. The core pragmatic principle is acknowledgment before advice — people in distress need to feel heard before they need solutions. Jumping to problem-solving too quickly signals impatience with the emotional moment.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Responding to a colleague who has just lost a family member.
- Scenario B: Consoling a friend who has just gone through a relationship ending.
- Scenario C: Expressing sympathy to an acquaintance who has experienced a professional setback.

---

### Intent 2.5.2 — Offering Emotional Support

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of Struggle] + [Presence/Availability Marker] + [Optional: Non-Directive Invitation to Share More]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Differs from sympathy in that it creates an ongoing supportive channel rather than acknowledging a specific event. The non-directive invitation ("I'm here if you want to talk") respects the hearer's autonomy to decide how much to share. Unsolicited advice at this stage is almost always counterproductive.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a friend who seems withdrawn and stressed that you are available and present for them.
- Scenario B: Supporting a family member going through a difficult medical process.
- Scenario C: Checking in on a colleague who has been visibly struggling at work.

---

### Intent 2.5.3 — Validating Someone's Feelings or Perspective

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Mirroring of Emotional Content] + [Normalization Marker] + [Optional: Shared Experience or Perspective]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
One of the most powerful rapport-building acts. Speaker does not need to agree with the hearer's position — only to acknowledge that the feeling or perspective is understandable given the hearer's experience. "I understand why you feel that way" is different from "I agree with you." Conflating the two creates relational problems.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Validating a friend's frustration with a situation even when you see both sides.
- Scenario B: Acknowledging a team member's concerns about a decision during a meeting.
- Scenario C: Telling a child that their fear or sadness about something makes sense.

---

### Intent 2.5.4 — Encouraging Someone

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of Difficulty or Doubt] + [Positive Capability Statement] + [Forward-Looking Motivation Marker]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker actively boosts hearer's confidence or motivation. Effective encouragement is specific and grounded — it names what the hearer is capable of, not just a generic "you can do it." Empty encouragement from someone who doesn't know the hearer's situation is quickly detected as hollow.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Encouraging a friend before a job interview or important presentation.
- Scenario B: Motivating a student who is discouraged after a poor test result.
- Scenario C: Encouraging a colleague to share their idea in a meeting they are nervous about.

---

### Intent 2.5.5 — Checking In on Someone's Wellbeing

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Reference to Person's State or Recent Event] + [Wellbeing Inquiry] + [Optional: Signal of Genuine Interest]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A proactive social bonding act. Signals that the speaker has been paying attention to the hearer's life. The reference to a specific recent event ("I know you had that difficult meeting last week...") is what separates a genuine check-in from a routine formulaic greeting.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Following up with a colleague after they went through a difficult professional period.
- Scenario B: Checking in on a friend after they mentioned dealing with a health issue.
- Scenario C: A manager proactively asking a team member how they are coping with a heavy workload.

---

## 2.6 Disagreement, Conflict & Difficult Confrontations

---

### Intent 2.6.1 — Expressing Disagreement (Soft)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Partial Agreement or Acknowledgment Marker] + [Contrast Marker] + [Counter-Perspective] + [Optional: Supporting Reasoning]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The partial agreement opener ("I see your point, but...") is a near-universal face-saving device that softens the disagreement and signals the speaker is engaging in good faith. Omitting it makes the disagreement feel like an attack. The contrast marker pivots to the speaker's actual position without dismissing the hearer's.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Gently disagreeing with a colleague's approach in a team discussion.
- Scenario B: Telling a friend you see a situation differently without starting a conflict.
- Scenario C: Respectfully challenging an assumption made during a group meeting.

---

### Intent 2.6.2 — Expressing Strong Disagreement or Objection

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Direct Negative Evaluation Marker] + [Specific Point of Contention] + [Evidence or Reasoning] + [Optional: Alternative Position]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker abandons softening in favor of clarity. Used when the stakes are too high for ambiguity — safety, ethics, significant errors. The speaker must accept the social risk of damaging rapport in service of accuracy or principle. Evidence strengthens the position; emotion without evidence weakens it.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Objecting clearly to a plan in a meeting that you believe is fundamentally flawed.
- Scenario B: Strongly disagreeing with a statement that contains factual errors with significant consequences.
- Scenario C: Refusing to agree to terms in a negotiation that are unacceptable.

---

### Intent 2.6.3 — Confronting Someone About Their Behavior

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Specific Behavior Description — Non-Judgmental] + [Impact Statement — First Person] + [Optional: Expectation or Request for Change]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The "I-statement" structure is pragmatically superior in almost all cultures because it grounds the confrontation in observable behavior and personal impact rather than character judgment. "You always do X" triggers defensiveness; "When X happens, I feel Y" invites dialogue. The confrontation must be specific — vague complaints are impossible to act on.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a colleague that their habit of interrupting you in meetings affects your ability to contribute.
- Scenario B: Raising with a friend that a recurring behavior makes you feel undervalued.
- Scenario C: Telling a partner that a specific pattern in their communication is causing you distress.

---

### Intent 2.6.4 — De-escalating a Conflict

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Emotional Acknowledgment] + [Pause/Space Marker] + [Common Ground Statement] + [Optional: Proposed Resolution Path]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Used when an interaction has become too heated for productive dialogue. The speaker prioritizes relationship preservation over winning the argument. Acknowledging the other party's emotional state first is the key — it signals that the speaker is not dismissing their perspective, only the escalation itself.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Calming a tense argument with a partner before it causes lasting damage.
- Scenario B: Mediating between two colleagues who are in open conflict in a workplace.
- Scenario C: Redirecting a heated discussion in a group meeting that has become personal.

---

### Intent 2.6.5 — Complaining About a Situation or Behavior

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Problem Statement] + [Frequency or Duration Marker] + [Impact on Speaker] + [Optional: Desired Change]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Complaining is a social bonding act when directed outward (complaining about a situation to a sympathetic listener) and a potentially face-threatening act when directed at the responsible party. The frequency marker ("every single time...") amplifies the complaint's legitimacy. Without a desired change, complaints are ventilation; with one, they are a request.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a building manager that a recurring maintenance issue is unresolved and affecting daily life.
- Scenario B: Venting to a trusted friend about a recurring frustration at work.
- Scenario C: Formally complaining to a service provider about repeated failures to meet a standard.

---

# MODULE 3: COGNITIVE PROCESSING & LOGIC

*The intellectual layer — how humans reason, speculate, report, and handle uncertainty and complex conditional thinking.*

---

## 3.1 Opinions, Beliefs & Certainty

---

### Intent 3.1.1 — Stating a Personal Opinion

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Opinion Framing Marker] + [Evaluative Statement] + [Optional: Supporting Reasoning]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The framing marker ("In my view...", "I think...") signals that what follows is a subjective position, not an objective fact. This is pragmatically important — presenting opinions as facts is a source of significant interpersonal conflict. The marker invites dialogue; the factual presentation of opinion shuts it down.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Sharing your view on a policy decision during a team discussion.
- Scenario B: Telling a friend what you think about a film, book, or decision they asked about.
- Scenario C: Expressing your perspective on a community issue at a local meeting.

---

### Intent 3.1.2 — Expressing Strong Certainty

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[High-Certainty Marker] + [Assertion] + [Optional: Evidence or Source]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker presents information as established and not open to negotiation. The evidential basis strengthens the claim — certainty without evidence can read as arrogance. Overusing certainty markers erodes credibility because hearers notice when claimed certainties prove wrong.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Confirming factual information you are sure of to a colleague who doubts it.
- Scenario B: Assuring a worried person that a situation is definitely under control.
- Scenario C: Asserting your own professional position with confidence in a meeting.

---

### Intent 3.1.3 — Expressing Uncertainty or Doubt

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Uncertainty Marker] + [Tentative Claim] + [Optional: Invitation for Correction or Verification]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Epistemic honesty. Signals the speaker is not fully confident and invites input. Culturally, admitting uncertainty is valued in high-trust, low-power-distance environments and seen as weakness in some high-status or hierarchical contexts. The speaker must calibrate this against the social cost of admitting not knowing.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Answering a question where you have partial but not complete knowledge.
- Scenario B: Sharing a prediction or plan that you are not fully confident will work.
- Scenario C: Expressing doubt about a piece of information before sharing it.

---

### Intent 3.1.4 — Agreeing with a Statement or Position

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Agreement Marker] + [Optional: Elaboration or Reinforcement] + [Optional: Reason or Personal Experience]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Positive alignment. The degree of elaboration signals the depth of agreement — "yes" alone is minimal; "yes, and in my experience..." is engaged. Agreements also function as bonding acts — they build solidarity and momentum in a conversation.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Affirming a colleague's diagnosis of a problem during a team discussion.
- Scenario B: Telling a friend you share their perspective on a social situation.
- Scenario C: Responding positively to a proposal in a meeting.

---

### Intent 3.1.5 — Partially Agreeing or Expressing a Qualified Position

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Partial Agreement Marker] + [Scope Qualifier — What You Agree With] + [Contrast Marker] + [Point of Divergence]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The most nuanced of the agreement/disagreement family. Speaker acknowledges the complexity of a position. This is the conversational move of the intellectually honest — it resists binary pressure to fully agree or fully disagree. It is also highly face-preserving for both parties.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Responding to a complex proposal that has both strong and weak elements.
- Scenario B: Giving feedback on a creative work that you find partially successful.
- Scenario C: Responding to a political or social opinion that you neither fully support nor fully oppose.

---

## 3.2 Speculation & Hypothetical Reasoning

---

### Intent 3.2.1 — Speculating About a Present or Future State

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Speculation Marker] + [Tentative Claim About Uncertain State] + [Optional: Evidence or Reasoning Behind Speculation]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker is reasoning beyond available evidence. The speculation marker is essential — without it, the claim reads as fact. Speculation is a collaborative act — good speculators invite hearers to engage with the uncertainty rather than simply presenting a guess as a conclusion.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Guessing why a person who hasn't arrived yet might be delayed.
- Scenario B: Speculating about what a competitor might do in response to a business decision.
- Scenario C: Estimating what outcome a decision is likely to produce based on limited information.

---

### Intent 3.2.2 — Forming a Hypothesis or Reasoned Conclusion

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Evidence Presentation] + [Logical Connector] + [Inferred Conclusion] + [Optional: Confidence Level Marker]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
More formal than speculation — speaker is reasoning from evidence to conclusion. Used in professional, academic, and problem-solving contexts. The logical connector is the hinge of the utterance — it shows the reasoning process, which allows others to challenge the inference rather than just the conclusion.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Explaining to a technician why you believe a specific component is causing a malfunction.
- Scenario B: Presenting a reasoned argument for why a project failed during a debrief.
- Scenario C: A doctor reasoning through a probable diagnosis based on observed symptoms.

---

### Intent 3.2.3 — Discussing a Hypothetical or Counterfactual Situation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Hypothetical Frame Marker] + [Counter-to-Fact Condition] + [Speculated Outcome] + [Optional: Implication or Lesson]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Exists entirely in an imagined, non-actual state. Used for planning ("what if X happens"), learning from past errors ("if I had done X instead"), and philosophical discussion. The hypothetical frame must be clearly established — confusing hypothetical with actual is a significant source of miscommunication.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Discussing with a team what would happen if a key project element were to fail.
- Scenario B: Reflecting with a friend on what your life might look like if you had made a different major decision.
- Scenario C: A trainer presenting a workplace emergency scenario to test staff responses.

---

### Intent 3.2.4 — Making a Conditional Proposal ("If... then...")

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Condition Marker] + [Required Condition] + [Resulting Proposition or Action]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Links two states causally or logically. Used heavily in negotiation ("If you can do X, then I will do Y"), problem-solving, planning, and instruction. The speaker is not committing to the outcome unconditionally — the condition is the lever of control. Hearers must process both parts to understand the full pragmatic meaning.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a contractor that if they meet the deadline, you will consider giving them future work.
- Scenario B: Explaining to a child that if they complete their responsibilities, they can have free time.
- Scenario C: Negotiating a deal by specifying conditions under which you will accept the terms.

---

## 3.3 Information Exchange — Reporting, Sourcing & Evidentiality

---

### Intent 3.3.1 — Reporting Second-Hand Information

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Source Attribution Marker] + [Reported Claim] + [Optional: Speaker's Own Stance on Reliability]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The speaker distances themselves from the claim by attributing it to another source. This is a critical information hygiene move — presenting reported information as one's own creates false authority. The reliability marker ("apparently," "supposedly," "I heard...") signals epistemic distance and invites the hearer to evaluate the claim accordingly.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Relaying to a colleague what you heard a manager say in a meeting you both didn't attend.
- Scenario B: Telling a friend what someone else told you about a shared acquaintance.
- Scenario C: Passing on a piece of news you read or heard without being able to verify it personally.

---

### Intent 3.3.2 — Citing a Source or Authority

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Source Identity Marker] + [Source's Claim or Finding] + [Optional: Speaker's Evaluation or Application]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker borrows epistemic authority from an external source. Strengthens the claim but also exposes the speaker to challenges about the source's credibility. In professional and academic contexts, sourcing is mandatory; in casual conversation, it adds weight to an argument.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Referencing a study or expert opinion to support a point in a discussion.
- Scenario B: Quoting an official policy or document to justify a decision.
- Scenario C: Telling someone what a doctor, lawyer, or specialist told you in order to inform their decision.

---

### Intent 3.3.3 — Asking for the Source of Information

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Reference to Claim Already Made] + [Source Inquiry Marker]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker exercises epistemic vigilance — they are not simply accepting the claim but evaluating its basis. In some cultures and contexts this is a natural part of discourse; in others it can read as a challenge to the speaker's honesty or competence. The tone of delivery is crucial.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking a colleague where they got a statistic they used in a presentation.
- Scenario B: Asking a friend how they found out about a piece of news they just shared.
- Scenario C: Asking a customer service representative which policy document they are referring to.

---

### Intent 3.3.4 — Sharing New or Surprising Information

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Attention Signal / News Marker] + [New Information] + [Optional: Reaction Invite]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker is introducing information the hearer does not yet have. The news marker ("Did you hear...?", "You won't believe...") primes the hearer for novelty and signals the speaker's own assessment of the information's significance. Omitting the marker can make genuinely surprising news sound flat.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a colleague an unexpected development in a shared project.
- Scenario B: Sharing an unexpected news item with a friend or family member.
- Scenario C: Informing a group of a sudden change in plans that affects everyone.

---

### Intent 3.3.5 — Correcting Misinformation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Softener/Politeness Opener] + [Reference to Incorrect Claim] + [Correction] + [Optional: Evidence or Source]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A face-threatening act directed at the hearer. The politeness opener is essential — without it, correction reads as humiliation. The speaker must signal that they are correcting the information, not attacking the person. In high-status or public contexts, corrections must be handled especially carefully.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Gently correcting a colleague who has stated an incorrect fact in a meeting.
- Scenario B: Correcting a family member who is acting on outdated or incorrect information about health or safety.
- Scenario C: Politely telling a service provider that the information they gave you was inaccurate.

---

## 3.4 Cause, Effect & Explanation

---

### Intent 3.4.1 — Explaining Cause or Reason

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Effect/Situation] + [Causal Connector] + [Cause/Reason] + [Optional: Chain of Causation]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker provides the causal logic behind a situation or decision. In professional contexts, the ability to trace causation clearly is a core competency. In interpersonal contexts, explaining your reasons transforms a bare statement ("I can't come") into a socially intelligible one ("I can't come because...").

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Explaining to a manager why a project milestone was missed.
- Scenario B: Telling a friend why you were unable to meet as planned.
- Scenario C: A teacher explaining to students why a particular approach or rule is in place.

---

### Intent 3.4.2 — Describing a Consequence or Outcome

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Cause or Action] + [Consequence Connector] + [Resulting Outcome] + [Optional: Magnitude or Significance Marker]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker traces what followed from an action or situation. Used in reporting, analysis, warning, and storytelling. The magnitude marker ("and as a result, everything changed") signals how significant the consequence is and guides the hearer's emotional and evaluative response.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Debriefing a team on what went wrong and what the downstream effects were.
- Scenario B: Explaining to a customer what will happen if a payment is not received by a deadline.
- Scenario C: Warning a child or colleague about the consequence of a particular action or inaction.

---

### Intent 3.4.3 — Drawing a Comparison

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Entity A] + [Comparison Marker] + [Entity B] + [Dimension of Comparison] + [Optional: Evaluative Conclusion]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker helps the hearer understand something unfamiliar by mapping it to something familiar, or evaluates two options against each other. Effective comparisons share the same dimension — comparing price to price, quality to quality. Mixing dimensions ("this one is cheaper but that one is more reliable") requires an explicit framework for the hearer to process.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Explaining an unfamiliar concept by comparing it to something the hearer already understands.
- Scenario B: Helping a customer choose between two products by comparing their features on the same dimensions.
- Scenario C: Comparing two job offers or life options with a friend who is helping you decide.

---

### Intent 3.4.4 — Warning About a Risk or Danger

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Attention Signal] + [Risk or Danger Description] + [Optional: Probability or Severity Marker] + [Recommended Action]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker exercises a duty of care. The attention signal is critical — it must be strong enough to break through the hearer's current focus. Over-warning (crying wolf) erodes the signal's power. The recommended action must follow the warning — a warning without a path forward generates anxiety without agency.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Warning a colleague that a process they are about to use has a known flaw that could cause errors.
- Scenario B: Alerting a traveler to a safety concern in a particular area or situation.
- Scenario C: Warning a family member that a decision they are considering carries significant risk.

# MODULE 4: METACOGNITIVE DISCOURSE MAINTENANCE

*The conversational management layer — how humans regulate, repair, and sustain communication itself.*

---

## 4.1 Comprehension, Clarification & Repair

---

### Intent 4.1.1 — Indicating Non-Comprehension

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Apology/Softener] + [Non-Comprehension Signal] + [Optional: Specific Point of Failure — Acoustic, Lexical, or Conceptual]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker acknowledges a breakdown in the communication channel. Identifying the type of failure helps the hearer provide a useful repair — "I didn't hear you" invites repetition; "I didn't understand" invites simplification or rephrasing; "I'm not sure what you mean by X" invites definition. Undifferentiated "huh?" forces the hearer to guess which repair strategy to use.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a native speaker that they spoke too quickly and you need them to repeat.
- Scenario B: Asking a doctor to explain a medical term you have not encountered before.
- Scenario C: Requesting that a presenter clarify an ambiguous point after a meeting.

---

### Intent 4.1.2 — Requesting Repetition (Acoustic Failure)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Apology Marker] + [Acoustic Failure Signal] + [Repetition Request] + [Optional: Partial Echo of What Was Heard]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Specific subtype of non-comprehension where the issue is hearing, not understanding. The partial echo ("I heard you say something about X...?") shows the hearer was paying attention and pins the exact moment of failure, which is more efficient than requesting a full repetition.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking someone to repeat a name, number, or address you did not catch clearly.
- Scenario B: Asking a person on a noisy phone call to repeat something that was lost to static.
- Scenario C: Requesting a speaker at a public event to repeat a statement that was obscured by crowd noise.

---

### Intent 4.1.3 — Requesting Clarification or Simplification

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of What Was Understood] + [Specific Unclear Element] + [Clarification Request] + [Optional: Proposed Interpretation for Confirmation]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
More cognitively demanding than repetition requests. Speaker understood the words but not the full meaning or implication. Proposing an interpretation ("Do you mean X?") is highly efficient — it invites a simple yes/no rather than a full re-explanation, and demonstrates the speaker's active engagement.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking a manager to clarify what they mean by a vague instruction.
- Scenario B: Asking a legal or technical professional to explain a complex concept in simpler terms.
- Scenario C: Checking with a friend what they meant by an ambiguous statement.

---

### Intent 4.1.4 — Verifying Your Own Understanding (Comprehension Check)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Paraphrase of What You Understood] + [Comprehension Verification Marker]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker actively confirms their interpretation is correct before acting on it. This closes the feedback loop in communication and prevents costly errors. It is especially critical in high-stakes instruction-following (medical, legal, technical). Culturally, some societies see this as a sign of diligence; others may interpret it as doubting the clarity of the original speaker.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Repeating back a set of instructions to a supervisor to confirm you have understood them correctly.
- Scenario B: Summarizing a client's requirements at the end of a discovery call to make sure nothing was missed.
- Scenario C: Checking with a speaker after a long explanation that you understood their main point correctly.

---

### Intent 4.1.5 — Checking Hearer Comprehension

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Information Delivery] + [Comprehension Check Probe]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker verifies that the hearer has understood, rather than assuming success. The phrasing must be genuinely inviting — "Does that make sense?" risks being heard as condescending, while "Is that clear, or would it help if I showed an example?" is more collaborative. The check should follow natural information chunks, not overwhelm the hearer by waiting until the end of a very long explanation.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Pausing during a complex technical explanation to verify the listener is following.
- Scenario B: A teacher checking student understanding after introducing a new concept.
- Scenario C: A customer service agent confirming a customer understood the steps they were just given.

---

### Intent 4.1.6 — Asking for a Definition or Explanation of a Term

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Term/Concept Reference] + [Definition Request] + [Optional: Context in Which You Encountered It]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker acknowledges a lexical or conceptual gap. Providing the context helps the hearer give a more targeted, useful definition. This is pragmatically more valuable than a decontextualized dictionary definition, which may not apply to the specific use the speaker encountered.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking a specialist to explain a technical term they used in a report or meeting.
- Scenario B: Asking a native speaker what a regional expression or slang term means.
- Scenario C: Asking a legal or financial professional what a specific term means in the context of a document you are reading.

---

## 4.2 Managing Conversational Flow

---

### Intent 4.2.1 — Opening a Conversation or Topic

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Attention Getter] + [Topic Introduction Marker] + [New Topic or Subject] + [Optional: Reason for Raising It]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The opener frames everything that follows. An abrupt topic introduction without a framing marker can disorient the hearer. In formal contexts, the reason for raising the topic is usually given first. In casual conversation, the attention getter alone is often sufficient.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Opening a team meeting by introducing the main agenda item.
- Scenario B: Starting a difficult personal conversation with a partner or family member.
- Scenario C: Breaking the ice with a stranger by introducing a conversational topic.

---

### Intent 4.2.2 — Changing the Subject

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Optional: Closure Marker for Current Topic] + [Topic Change Signal] + [New Topic]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Abrupt topic changes violate conversational coherence and can feel dismissive. The closure marker ("On a different note...", "Anyway...") signals the speaker is consciously redirecting rather than simply ignoring what was said. In some contexts, topic changes are used to escape uncomfortable discussions — hearers are usually aware of this.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Redirecting a conversation that has reached a natural end point.
- Scenario B: Shifting away from a topic that is becoming too sensitive or emotional.
- Scenario C: Steering a meeting back on agenda after a prolonged tangential discussion.

---

### Intent 4.2.3 — Interrupting Politely

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Apology/Softener] + [Interruption Signal] + [Reason for Interruption or Quick Point]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Interruption is a face-threatening act — it signals that the speaker's need temporarily outweighs the hearer's right to complete their turn. The softener is essential. Legitimate interruption reasons include urgency, time constraints, and clarification of a critical point. Interrupting simply to dominate the conversation is universally recognized as socially inappropriate.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Interrupting a speaker in a meeting to flag that the group is running out of time.
- Scenario B: Cutting in during a conversation to clarify a critical misunderstanding before it compounds.
- Scenario C: Interrupting a phone call to say you are about to lose signal or must go.

---

### Intent 4.2.4 — Taking the Floor / Initiating a Turn

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Transition Signal] + [Optional: Acknowledgment of Previous Speaker] + [New Contribution]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
In multi-party conversations, claiming a speaking turn requires explicit signaling in many cultural contexts. Simply speaking louder is a low-status or high-aggression strategy. In some cultures, direct eye contact with the current speaker and a slight lean forward signal a desire to take the floor; in others, a verbal token is required.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Joining a group discussion where several people are contributing simultaneously.
- Scenario B: Introducing your point in a formal meeting after the current speaker has finished.
- Scenario C: Re-entering a conversation after being interrupted, to complete your original point.

---

### Intent 4.2.5 — Closing a Conversation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Summary or Final Point Marker] + [Closure Signal] + [Farewell or Next-Step Marker]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A conversation that is closed abruptly leaves the hearer feeling dismissed. The pre-closing sequence (summary, signal, farewell) gives both parties time to surface any remaining points before the interaction ends. In professional contexts, the next-step marker ("I'll follow up by Thursday") transforms closure into a commitment.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Ending a phone call with a professional contact after the business has been concluded.
- Scenario B: Wrapping up a social visit without making the exit feel sudden or rude.
- Scenario C: Closing a meeting and summarizing action points before participants leave.

---

### Intent 4.2.6 — Stalling for Processing Time

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Filler / Hesitation Marker] + [Optional: Restatement of the Question to Buy Time]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Every fluent speaker uses time-buying devices while processing. These are not failures — they are signals to the hearer that the speaker is actively thinking, not ignoring them. In a second language, the ability to use target-language stalling devices is a significant fluency milestone, as it prevents the speaker from defaulting to silence or their first language.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Pausing to think through a complex question in a job interview.
- Scenario B: Taking a moment before responding to an unexpected piece of news.
- Scenario C: Buying time in a negotiation before responding to an offer you need to evaluate.

---

### Intent 4.2.7 — Returning to a Previous Topic

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Return Marker] + [Reference to Earlier Topic] + [New Point or Continuation]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Speaker re-anchors the conversation to an earlier point that was left unresolved or deserves more attention. The reference marker ensures the hearer can locate the topic in conversational memory. Without it, the new point can seem disconnected.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Returning to an agenda item in a meeting that was deferred earlier.
- Scenario B: Coming back to a point you wanted to make in a conversation after being interrupted.
- Scenario C: Revisiting an earlier topic in a negotiation that seemed closed but now needs reconsideration.

---

## 4.3 Repair, Correction & Precision

---

### Intent 4.3.1 — Self-Correcting Misspoken Information

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Self-Interruption Marker] + [Correction] + [Continuation]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Immediate self-repair is a sign of monitoring and precision. It signals to the hearer that accuracy matters to the speaker. Failing to self-correct after noticing an error can damage credibility. The self-interruption marker ("or rather...", "I mean...") cues the hearer to mentally replace the previous statement.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Correcting a date, name, or number you stated incorrectly in a presentation.
- Scenario B: Self-correcting a word choice in conversation when you realize it conveyed the wrong meaning.
- Scenario C: Correcting a claim you made in a meeting as soon as you realize it was inaccurate.

---

### Intent 4.3.2 — Paraphrasing to Confirm Understanding

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Paraphrase Marker] + [Restatement of Hearer's Point in Speaker's Own Words] + [Confirmation Invite]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Active listening made audible. By restating the hearer's point in different words, the speaker demonstrates comprehension and gives the hearer the opportunity to correct any misreading. Essential in negotiation, counseling, teaching, and any high-stakes dialogue where misunderstanding has significant consequences.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Paraphrasing a client's needs back to them during a project scoping meeting.
- Scenario B: Restating a friend's problem back to them during a supportive conversation.
- Scenario C: Summarizing a speaker's argument in your own words to check you have understood it correctly before responding.

---

### Intent 4.3.3 — Asking for Spelling, Writing, or Pronunciation Clarification

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Target Word/Name] + [Specific Clarification Type — Spelling/Pronunciation/Script] + [Inquiry Marker]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Operational necessity — especially critical for names, technical terms, addresses, and medication names. Getting a name wrong is a significant face-threatening error; confirming spelling beforehand prevents it. In a second language context, asking for pronunciation is a basic but essential act of language-learning communication.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Asking someone to spell their name slowly so you can write it down correctly.
- Scenario B: Asking how to pronounce an unfamiliar word in the language you are learning.
- Scenario C: Requesting the correct written form of an address or technical term to avoid transcription errors.

---

### Intent 4.3.4 — Signaling That More Information Will Follow

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Preview Marker] + [Topic or Number of Points to Come] + [Optional: Structural Map]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The speaker primes the hearer for incoming information. Providing a structural map ("I have three points...") significantly improves a hearer's ability to process and retain what follows. Without it, listeners cannot tell when one point ends and the next begins. Used heavily in presentations, explanations, and storytelling.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Introducing a complex explanation in a meeting by previewing its structure.
- Scenario B: Telling a customer service agent that you have several issues to raise before beginning.
- Scenario C: Starting a story or account by setting up the framework of what you are about to say.

---

### Intent 4.3.5 — Summarizing What Was Said

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Summary Marker] + [Condensed Restatement of Key Points] + [Optional: Action Items or Next Steps]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Performs a closure and consolidation function. Ensures that all parties leave the conversation with the same understanding. In professional settings, the person who summarizes often shapes what the group takes away — summarizing is therefore a subtle form of influence. In learning contexts, summarizing is a comprehension demonstration.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Summarizing the decisions made at the end of a team meeting.
- Scenario B: Wrapping up a phone consultation by restating what was discussed and agreed.
- Scenario C: Summarizing your understanding of an argument or presentation after listening to it.

# MODULE 5: IDENTITY, RELATIONSHIPS & SOCIAL POSITIONING

*How humans present themselves, manage social roles, and navigate the full range of relationship types.*

---

## 5.1 Self-Presentation & Identity

---

### Intent 5.1.1 — Introducing Yourself

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Name] + [Role/Affiliation] + [Optional: Purpose for Interaction] + [Optional: Relationship-Building Opener]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
First impression management. The role or affiliation establishes the social frame for the entire interaction. In professional contexts, name and role are the minimum; in social contexts, adding a personal detail humanizes the interaction. Cultural norms on directness and formality of self-introduction vary significantly.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Introducing yourself to a new colleague on your first day at work.
- Scenario B: Introducing yourself to a stranger at a social event or gathering.
- Scenario C: Introducing yourself to a client or partner at the start of a professional meeting.

---

### Intent 5.1.2 — Introducing Someone Else

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Attention to Both Parties] + [Person A's Name/Role] + [Connection to Context] + [Person B's Name/Role] + [Optional: Shared Interest or Reason for Introduction]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The introducer acts as a social bridge. A good introduction gives both parties something to start a conversation with — simply stating names is the minimum. Adding a shared connection or relevant context ("You both work in data") gives the new pair a conversational foothold.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Introducing two colleagues from different departments who will now be working together.
- Scenario B: Introducing a friend to your partner at a social event.
- Scenario C: Formally introducing a guest speaker to an audience before a presentation.

---

### Intent 5.1.3 — Describing Yourself (Background, Profession, Interests)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Current Role/Status] + [Background Summary] + [Optional: Key Interests or Values] + [Optional: Goal or Reason for Sharing]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A selective act — people share what is relevant and flattering to the context. Professional descriptions emphasize competence and role; social descriptions emphasize personality and interests. Oversharing in a first interaction is universally experienced as uncomfortable.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Describing your professional background in a job interview.
- Scenario B: Answering "tell me about yourself" in a social or networking context.
- Scenario C: Filling in a new acquaintance on your current life situation.

---

### Intent 5.1.4 — Stating Personal Values or Principles

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Value/Principle Statement] + [Optional: Behavioral Manifestation] + [Optional: Origin or Reason]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A high-trust disclosure. Stating your values makes you legible and predictable to others, which builds trust. It also signals where your non-negotiable lines are. Overstating values without demonstrated behavioral consistency backfires — hearers notice the gap between stated principles and actions.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Explaining to a business partner what principles guide your professional decisions.
- Scenario B: Telling a new acquaintance what you stand for in the context of a relevant discussion.
- Scenario C: Articulating your values to a child or mentee as a form of guidance.

---

## 5.2 Relationship Dynamics & Social Roles

---

### Intent 5.2.1 — Expressing Respect or Deference to Authority

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Honorific/Formality Marker] + [Content] + [Optional: Deferential Closing]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Social hierarchy maintenance. Speakers signal awareness of power differentials through formality level, address forms, indirect constructions, and hedging. The specific markers are language-specific, but the underlying universal is: speakers calibrate their register to the perceived social distance and relative power of the hearer.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Addressing a senior government official or dignitary in a formal setting.
- Scenario B: Speaking to an elder or grandparent in a culture with strong age hierarchy norms.
- Scenario C: Presenting formally to an executive or board in a professional context.

---

### Intent 5.2.2 — Expressing Solidarity or In-Group Membership

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Shared Reference/Informal Marker] + [Content] + [Optional: Collaborative Frame]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Register shift downward signals closeness and equality. Using informal language, shared references, or in-group vocabulary with the right person strengthens the bond; using them with the wrong person signals disrespect or social ignorance. Solidarity markers are some of the most culturally specific elements of language.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Using casual register and shared references with a close colleague versus a formal one.
- Scenario B: Speaking to a close friend using in-group language that signals membership in a shared community.
- Scenario C: A sports fan speaking to another fan using team-specific references and informal language.

---

### Intent 5.2.3 — Asking About Someone's Life, Family, or Situation

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Reference to Person/Topic] + [Inquiry Marker] + [Optional: Last Known Situation as Context]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Social maintenance. Remembering details from a previous interaction and asking about them is one of the most powerful relational acts — it signals that the person matters enough to be remembered. Generic questions ("How are you?") are largely phatic; specific questions ("How did your presentation go?") demonstrate genuine engagement.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Following up with a colleague about a family event they mentioned in a previous conversation.
- Scenario B: Asking a friend about a health situation you knew they were dealing with.
- Scenario C: Asking a long-absent acquaintance to catch you up on their life since you last spoke.

---

### Intent 5.2.4 — Expressing Commitment or Loyalty

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Relationship Reference] + [Commitment Declaration] + [Optional: Behavioral Evidence or Promise]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Relational anchor-setting. Speaker explicitly affirms a bond and signals their reliability within it. Particularly important after conflict or periods of distance. The behavioral evidence component is crucial — declarations without actions are recognized as empty across all cultures.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Assuring a partner of your commitment after a period of doubt or conflict.
- Scenario B: Pledging your support to a colleague who is in a professional crisis.
- Scenario C: Affirming loyalty to a team or organization during a period of uncertainty.

# MODULE 6: NARRATIVE, CREATIVE & PERFORMATIVE COMMUNICATION

*How humans tell stories, entertain, persuade, and perform social rituals through language.*

---

## 6.1 Storytelling & Narrative

---

### Intent 6.1.1 — Narrating a Past Event

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Scene/Time Setting] + [Character/Agent Introduction] + [Sequential Event Chain] + [Resolution or Outcome] + [Optional: Moral or Reflection]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The fundamental human communicative act. All cultures narrate — the structural variation is in how explicitly the moral is stated, how much scene-setting is expected, and how linearly or non-linearly the events are presented. High-context cultures often embed the point; low-context cultures state it explicitly.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Telling a friend about a significant experience you had recently.
- Scenario B: Recounting an event to a family member who was not present.
- Scenario C: Telling a colleague what happened during a situation they asked about.

---

### Intent 6.1.2 — Telling a Joke or Humorous Anecdote

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Setup — Establishing Normal Context] + [Punchline — Unexpected Subversion of Context] + [Optional: Reaction Invite]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Humor is one of the most culturally variable and socially risky speech acts. The core mechanism is universal (setup + subversion of expectation), but what counts as an acceptable or funny subversion is deeply cultural. Humor creates intense in-group solidarity when it lands and significant social damage when it fails.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Breaking the ice with a new group using a light, universally accessible humorous observation.
- Scenario B: Diffusing tension in a meeting or difficult conversation with appropriate humor.
- Scenario C: Entertaining friends or family with a humorous story from personal experience.

---

### Intent 6.1.3 — Describing a Person

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Person Reference] + [Physical Description — Optional] + [Personality/Character Description] + [Role or Relationship] + [Optional: Evaluative Summary]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The balance between physical and character description is culturally and contextually determined. Describing a person you are recommending to someone favors character; describing someone you haven't seen in a long time favors physical identifiers. Evaluative summaries ("she's the most reliable person I've ever worked with") carry persuasive weight.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Describing a mutual acquaintance to someone who has not met them.
- Scenario B: Describing a job candidate you are recommending to a hiring manager.
- Scenario C: Helping someone identify a person they are supposed to meet in a crowd.

---

### Intent 6.1.4 — Describing an Object or Place

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Category Identification] + [Key Distinguishing Attributes] + [Function or Significance] + [Optional: Sensory Details]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The level of detail required is determined by the hearer's purpose — someone about to use the object needs functional information; someone about to buy it needs comparative attributes; someone who is simply curious can receive a richer sensory description. Speakers who misjudge the hearer's need for detail either bore or frustrate.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Describing a place you have visited to someone considering going there.
- Scenario B: Describing a lost item in enough detail for someone to help you find it.
- Scenario C: Describing a product you own to someone who asked for your recommendation.

---

## 6.2 Persuasion & Influence

---

### Intent 6.2.1 — Making an Argument or Case

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Position Statement] + [Supporting Evidence/Reason 1] + [Supporting Evidence/Reason 2] + [Optional: Acknowledgment of Counter-Position] + [Conclusion/Call to Agreement]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Structured persuasion. The acknowledgment of the counter-position is the mark of a sophisticated persuader — it signals intellectual honesty and preemptively defuses the strongest objection. Arguments that ignore counter-evidence are less persuasive to critical hearers.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Arguing for a specific approach or decision in a team or organizational context.
- Scenario B: Persuading a family member or friend to consider a different course of action.
- Scenario C: Making the case for a project proposal to a decision-making audience.

---

### Intent 6.2.2 — Appealing to Emotion

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Emotional Anchor — Shared Value or Experience] + [Stakes or Impact Framing] + [Call to Action or Alignment]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Rational argument persuades the mind; emotional appeal moves people to act. The most effective persuasion combines both. The emotional anchor must be genuine — manufactured or manipulative emotional appeals are quickly recognized and damage trust. The shared value framing ("we all care about...") invites the hearer into alignment rather than demanding it.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Motivating a team to push through a difficult period by appealing to shared purpose.
- Scenario B: Persuading a skeptical family member by framing an issue in terms of values they hold.
- Scenario C: Making a case for a charitable cause by personalizing the human impact.

---

### Intent 6.2.3 — Persuading Someone to Change Their Mind

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Acknowledgment of Current Position] + [New Evidence or Perspective] + [Reframe] + [Optional: Low-Pressure Invitation to Reconsider]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
One of the most socially delicate acts — changing your mind publicly has a face cost. Effective persuaders give the other person a narrative by which they can change positions without feeling humiliated ("given this new information, it makes sense to reconsider"). Forcing a reversal without this narrative rarely works.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Presenting new evidence to a colleague who made a decision based on incomplete information.
- Scenario B: Helping a family member reconsider a plan that you believe is harmful to them.
- Scenario C: Presenting a counter-argument in a debate or group discussion.

---

## 6.3 Social Rituals & Performative Language

---

### Intent 6.3.1 — Making a Toast or Public Tribute

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Attention Call] + [Subject Introduction] + [Tribute Content — Specific and Personal] + [Closing Affirmation or Wish]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
A performative speech act — the saying of it constitutes the social act. The specificity of the tribute content determines its emotional impact. Generic toasts are appreciated; specific, personal ones are remembered. The speaker temporarily takes on a ceremonial authority that the group grants.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Giving a toast at a wedding, retirement, or milestone birthday celebration.
- Scenario B: Publicly acknowledging a colleague's contribution at a team event.
- Scenario C: Offering a tribute at a memorial or farewell gathering.

---

### Intent 6.3.2 — Congratulating Someone on an Achievement

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Congratulation Marker] + [Specific Achievement] + [Optional: Acknowledgment of Effort or Journey] + [Optional: Future Wish]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The specificity of the achievement named signals genuine awareness versus formulaic politeness. Acknowledging the effort ("I know how hard you worked for this") adds depth. The future wish component is forward-looking and extends the positive frame beyond the achievement itself.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Congratulating a colleague on a promotion or professional achievement.
- Scenario B: Congratulating a friend on a personal milestone — graduation, marriage, new child.
- Scenario C: Publicly acknowledging a team's achievement after a successful project completion.

---

### Intent 6.3.3 — Wishing Someone Well (Good Luck, Get Well, Safe Journey)

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Wish Marker] + [Specific Good Outcome Wished] + [Optional: Sincere Personal Note]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
Phatic communion at a transitional moment. The speaker acknowledges that the hearer is entering a period of uncertainty or challenge and signals solidarity. The personal note elevates the wish from formulaic to sincere. Timing matters — the wish must precede the event, not follow it.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Wishing someone good luck before an important exam, interview, or performance.
- Scenario B: Wishing someone a safe journey before they travel.
- Scenario C: Wishing someone a speedy recovery when they are ill.

---

### Intent 6.3.4 — Expressing Celebration or Shared Joy

**Structural Semantic Blueprint:**
`[Exclamation or Enthusiasm Marker] + [Shared Achievement or Event] + [Optional: Invitation to Collective Positive Response]`

**Pragmatic Context:**
The speaker performs and amplifies positive emotion to create a shared emotional experience. This is contagious if genuine and hollow if performative. Group celebrations bond communities — the collective nature of the expression ("We did it!") is often more important than its literal content.

**Universal Target Scenarios:**
- Scenario A: Celebrating a sports team's victory with fellow fans.
- Scenario B: Sharing in a collective celebration at a professional milestone achieved together.
- Scenario C: Expressing shared joy at a family member's good news.
